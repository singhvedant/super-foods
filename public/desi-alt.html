Go homegrown and get back to the basics


    Few Desi-Alternatives are:
  • Jamun instead of Blueberries
  • Pumpkin Seeds instead of Walnut
  • Flax/Kuttu (Buckwheat) instead of Oats
  • Dill leaves (Sevu) instead of Seaweed
  • Sardines instead of Wild Salmon
  • Dahi instead of Kefir
  • Guava instead of Kiwi
  • Palak (Spinach) instead of Asparagus

Jamun instead of Blueberries

Blueberries from America contain resveratrol, a wonder substance that delays ageing and keeps your blood vessels healthy. You get fibre, vitamin C, vitamin K, manganese and potassium in every handful of blueberries. They’re also low in sodium and have virtually no fat. Blueberries are believed to have one of the highest antioxidant levels of all common fruits and vegetables. Resveratrol is found in bright red, blue, to violet fruits such as Jamun. Similarly, raspberries can be replaced with strawberries for the same anti-inflammatory and heart-protective pigment. You can also have peanuts, pistachios, cocoa and dark chocolate for resveratrol.

Pumpkin Seeds instead of Walnut

Agreed that this pricey tree nut spread across from Asia to America has the highest alpha-linolenic acid [omega-3]. But to give it a tough competition, we have the humble, inexpensive pumpkin seeds, which are mostly discarded. These miniatures are ample in monounsaturated fatty acids, magnesium that reduces blood pressure, and male virility boosting zinc.

Flax/Kuttu (Buckwheat) instead of Oats

The European crop is the best source of soluble fibre that cuts blood sugar spikes, eradicates cholesterol, controls weight and diabetes. You can trade it for flax for the same gain. A typical fasting food in Gujarat, buckwheat, aka Kuttu, is richer in magnesium and potassium than oats. A good source of complete protein, buckwheat contains all eight amino acids and helps improve gut health and blood circulation in your system

Dill leaves (Sevu) instead of Seaweed

The star is an alga growing wild along the shores [Nori, Kelp, sea lettuce], a powerhouse of nutrients with omega-3, calcium, magnesium, potassium, chlorophyll, vitamin B12, and high fibre. However, it seems to be an acquired taste. A similar flavourful herb is Dill leaves, also known as “Sevu”, with antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory influence with identical goodness. Fresh dill sprigs are intrinsically blessed with an array of nutrients including Vitamin A, C, D, riboflavin, manganese, folate, iron, copper, potassium, magnesium, zinc and dietary fibres. It Regulates Diabetes, Fortifies Bone Health Remedies Insomnia, Prevents Infections, Remedies Insomnia, and improves the general health of a person.

Sardines instead of Wild Salmon

Wild salmon from the Alaskan region has the most potent marine omega 3, with the benefit of the lowest mercury contamination and vitamin D. Vitamin D offers benefits from the bone to brain with protection against diabetes, cardiovascular and cancer. An Indian fish alternative like Sardines has the equivalent essential fatty acid minus mercury. It is rich in protein. It reduces inflammation and the risk of disease. Sardines are an excellent source of vitamin B 12, vitamin D, calcium, and selenium. Sardines contain a high content of omega-3 fatty acids. New findings indicate that there is a strong correlation between omega-3 fatty acids and a lack of depression.

Dahi instead of Kefir

The fermented milk originated from the North Caucasus mountains in Russia. The creamy, tangy beverage is prepared from the kefir culture, which is grainy and a blend of yeast and lactobacilli (bacteria). The drink with a characteristic flavour offers probiotics. The Indian Dahi is a champion to boast of identical perks. Curd/Dahi is the lightest and most-effective fuel you can rely on during summers. It can keep you hydrated and boost your energy levels at the same time. Curd is packed with the healthiest bacteria, which makes it a brilliant gut healer. Its probiotic nature improves the immunity system, and can potentially help your body fight everything from viral fevers to the common colds and infections. Dahi is also good for every kind of gut issue, from indigestion to bloating. Add a little salt or sugar, and your body will feel more balanced and energised. Dahi is also a great stress-buster and lowers anxiety levels. Which is exactly why Indian Mom’s keep insisting on consuming Dahi before exams. Dahi Chinni Nahi Khayenge toh goodluck Kese milenge?

Daliya (Broken wheat)/ Kuttu (Buckwheat)/ Amaranth instead of Quinoa

Quinoa is a superfood known for its high-fibre, high-protein seed, often mistaken for grain due to its texture when cooked. Quinoa has been a significant part of the diet in South American countries. There are a few alternatives to quinoa that are readily available, including Daliya (Broken/bulgur wheat), which contains nutrients including iron, folate, copper, niacin, magnesium, etc. Dalia is rich in fibre and can prevent overeating, just like oats. This cereal grain promotes your gut health and prevents the onset of constipation, a factor behind weight gain.

Kuttu, or buckwheat, is an alternative that has vitamins and is rich in fibre, along with magnesium, protein, and iron. It also lowers bad cholesterol in the body and is low in calories.

Amaranth is another local counterpart to quinoa, has been largely ignored. Also known as Rajgira or Ramdana—which loosely translates to the food of the Gods—it is considered one of the more essential foods of India, as per Ayurveda. Calcium, zinc, iron and fibre—Amaranth scores higher than quinoa on these properties. Plus, it's so much more pocket friendly.

Sabja (Basil seeds) instead of Chia seeds

Supermarket aisles and smoothie bowls--it’s hard to miss chia seeds these days. With origins in Southern American, the superfood rose to Internet fame in the past ten years. The seeds are rich in antioxidants and help with weight loss. They are, also, painfully expensive. Luckily, we have an Indian alternative to chia seeds--sweet Basil seeds, or better known, Sabja seeds. Basil seeds are an easy source of vitamins, iron, helps control blood sugar levels and fight seasonal flu. They're also perfect for your gut and can relieve constipation and bloating. Widely grown in India and other subtropical regions, sabja have many similar qualities to chia, help cool our bodies, and are a fourth of the price. There's an actual reason why falooda is a summer drink. Use them exactly as you would chia seeds; just remember that they have to be soaked and cannot be eaten as is.

Coconut/Mustard Oil instead of Olive oil

A recent wave declared olive oil the healthiest, low-cholesterol oil to cook foods in. The Mediterranean oil has taken the prime spot in most recipes and replaced our usual Indian oils, butter and even, sometimes ghee. But it has been found that our locally produced oils, such as coconut, mustard, peanut or even sesame, have similar health benefits as olive oil. Coconut Oil, the base of most South Indian foods, has recently even been identified as a superfood in the west. Likewise, Mustard Oil reduces inflammation, has antimicrobial benefits, and has a high smoke point. Good news for us; let’s get back to the basics.

Palak (Spinach) instead of Kale

You can pass a chip aisle without spotting dried kale chips. Kale, called a ‘super green’, is packed with antioxidants and vitamins. In India, we have spinach or palak, a powerhouse of nutrients with its rich magnesium, vitamin A, E, zinc, folate, and potassium content – essential for the fat-burning process and great health. Beetroot greens that we usually throw away is a high nutrition food waiting to be explored. Low in calories but high in Vitamin E, they're an excellent source of potassium.
